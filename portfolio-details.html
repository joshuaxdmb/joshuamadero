Marathon aircraft: what if humans could really fly?

  • Skills demonstrated: Aerodynamics, project management, mechanical design, electronics design, composites manufacturing.
  • Tools used: Xfoil, Xrotor, Solidworks, Arduino, C, Python
  • Company/Team: University of Toronto HPVDT
  • Project date: 2020-Present
  • Competition Information: The Kremer International Marathon Competition

My friends and I were looking for a challenging project to test and continue developing our skills designing aerodynamic shapes and composite structures, and nothing got us more excited than the Kremer International Marathon Competition by the Royal Aeronautical Society. This challenge consists of developing a human-powered aircraft (HPA) to travel along a specified 40-km path in less than one hour.

My primary role in this project is to design and build the propulsion system - a detailed description of this is shown in another section. Apart from that, I worked closely with my two co-leads to choose the configuration design; we opted for a tandem style where two cyclists provide power to a rear-mounted propeller. I am also designing the in-flight electronic system along with two other team members - more details in another section.

As team manager, I have to coordinate tasks and review the designs of other team members. I am also working with the faculty to develop a safety plan.

TITAN: The fastest tandem speedbike ever built.

  • Skills demonstrated: Mechanical design, electronic design, composites manufacturing.
  • Tools used: Solidworks, Arduino
  • Company/Team: University of Toronto HPVDT
  • Project date: 2019
  • Video URL: https://youtu.be/uYFdVVKSzns

At hpvdt.skule.ca, we build carbon fibre bicycles that break world records of speed and engineering while promoting sustainable transportation. In September of 2019, Titan travelled at 74mph, becoming the fastest tandem in history at the World Speed Challenge.

My main work with Titan was designing speedometers, using GPS and light sensors, as well as creating housings to secure the electronic system to the bike. This vehicles is fully closed, and the cyclist relies on cameras for visibility, so it is important to keep cameras and displays secure and stable. I also built moulds, composite structures, and jigs.

The most efficient low-speed propeller ever.

  • Skills demonstrated: Aerodynamic design, mechanical design, composites manufacturing.
  • Tools used: Xrotor, Xfoil, Solidworks, Python
  • Company/Team: University of Toronto HPVDT
  • Project date: 2020 - Present

My team is competing for the Kremer International Marathon Prize, and I am designing our propeller. I started this project knowing close to nothing about propellers and very little about aerodynamic design. However, over the past 12 months, I have learned as much as I could and created a design tool to find the most sustainable propeller configuration and shape for our design conditions.

This propeller design tool combines Python, Xfoil, Xrotor, and Solidworks. The input is a desired flight speed, thrust, candidate airfoils to analyze, and other few parameters. The output is a Solidworks macro to design a blade of the most efficient propeller possible for the selected configuration. I am curently building a prototype to validate the results.

Cooling shroud for metal powder reactor.

  • Skills demonstrated: Mechanical design, design for manufacturing, CFD.
  • Tools used: Solidworks, Ansys
  • Company/Team: University of Toronto CACT Laboratory
  • Project date: 2020 - 2021

The Center for Adcanved Coating Technologies (CACT) developes state-of-the-art thermal spraying techniques. They were lacking an effective cooling system for their reactor used to produce metal powder. This was important because the cooling process directly impacts the quality of the powder. Our design distributes nitrogen evenly and at high speeds inside the chamber, increasing the cooling rate and reducing contact with oxygen, which in turn reduces oxidation and results in smaller (better) particles.

The work was distributed evenly among the three members of my team. I did part of the design in Solidworks, and all of the fluid simulations. The design was built by a university shop and tested by my team at the CACT facility.

Zephyr: upright bike beats all other speedbikes at ASME E-fest.

  • Skills demonstrated: Aerodynamic design, composites manufacturing.
  • Tools used: Solidworks
  • Company/Team: University of Toronto HPVDT
  • Project date: 2018 - 2019

My team realized that the ASME E-Fest HPV Competitions were increasingly focusing on practicality and maneuverability rather than speed. However, all the teams continued to build aerodynamically optimized recumbent bikes, a configuration that is not adequate for this design objectives. We decided to change our strategy and built a 'typical' upright bike instead, while still trying to reduce drag via fairings that covered most of the cyclist frontal area. This worked well. We won't every athletic event and got second place overall.

My role in this project was to ensure our biked was adequately sized for our cyclists of various heights. I designed a realistic human model with variable height and body proportions to accomplish this goal. I also used this model to simulate the drag created by the body. I used Ansys Fluent to analyze the effects of the fairings and reduced drag by 30% from the first to the last iteration - this was validated with a prototype.

Accelerated lifecycle testig robot.

  • Skills demonstrated: Aerodynamic design, composites manufacturing.
  • Tools used: Solidworks
  • Company/Team: University of Toronto HPVDT
  • Project date: 2018 - 2019

At KitchenMate, I was part of the product development team working on the next-generation smart cooking appliance. We were concerned with the performance of the electronic devices that moved during normal operation. Previously, we had manually tested the long-time performance of the appliance in our office, but this tool several weeks. To speed up this process, I created a robot to actuate the machine, simulating a user, while the software was running. This allowed us to test over 35,000 cycles - more than the expected use over 5 yeras - in just a few days. The speed of the tests was limited by the software. This revealed multiple sources of failure, which we mitigated in later iterations.

For this project, I used an arduino, DC motor, and motor driver. The machine was connected to the smart cooker to detecto when the door was closed. The Arduino monitored the status of the smart cooker components, and the test would stop if any component fails.
